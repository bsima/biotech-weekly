Issue #4

This week in biotechnology: We have a new full color map to aid in our quest for biotech knowledge, and it comes from analyzing how cells talk using ligands. A 3D printed glove for the heart can keep it beating indefinitely, and the Google+Novartis team is making patented strides with their smart contact lenses. In tissue engineering, stem cell factories are becoming a thing. Paired with our new found ability to edit the genome of stem cells, this could mean great things for the future of personalized and regenerative medicine.

I really enjoyed putting together this issue. Loads of cool tissue engineering developments this week, my favorite area of research!

Happy Sciencing!

A dictionary of the language of cells // RIKEN

Scientists from the RIKEN Center for Life Science Technologies has published an overall map of how the cells in the human body communicate by systematically analyzing the relationship between ligands—substances such as insulin and interferon that embody messages between cells, and receptors—the proteins on cell surfaces that receive these messages when bound by the ligands.

Static synapses on a moving structure // King's College London

A new study published in PNAS by researchers from the MRC Centre for Developmental Neurobiology characterises a novel way in which neurons remain electrically stable when confronted with chronic increases in neuronal activity.

Diagnostic test developed for enterovirus D68 // Washington University in St. Louis

Researchers at Washington University School of Medicine in St. Louis have developed a diagnostic test to quickly detect enterovirus D68 (EV-D68), a respiratory virus that caused unusually severe illness in children last year. Tiny segments of viral DNA sequences that are common to every D68 subtype but no other virus were isolated and analyzed using a computer program that allows large numbers of DNA sequences to be compared simultaneously.

Development and Evaluation of an Enterovirus D68 Real-Time Reverse Transcriptase Polymerase Chain Reaction (RT-PCR) Assay

Tools and Gadgets

3D-printed 'electronic glove' could help keep your heart beating for ever // The Independent

Using a “spider-web-like network of sensors and electrodes” to continuously monitor the heart’s electrical activity, this cardiac sock has been custom fit to a rabbit heart with the use of computer modelling technology and a 3D-printer. The prototype membrane has kept the organ and operational functionality outside of the body in a nutrient and oxygen-rich solution.

Device keeps donor liver healthy outside body until transplant // UHN

A deceased donor liver has been successfully transplanted after being preserved and kept healthy outside the body in a device that mimics human physiological functions.The technique and device are part of a Phase 1 clinical trial at UHN's Toronto General Hospital assessing the safety of the device, with subsequent phases examining its efficacy.

Tissue Engineering

New material forges the way for 'stem cell factories' // The University of Nottingham

Researchers at The University of Nottingham have discovered the first fully synthetic substrate with potential to grow billions of stem cells. The scalable and cost-effective synthetic polymer substrate can be applied to common cultureware and used off-the-shelf after long-term storage.

You should start your stem cell banking now if you want to live forever.

Human heart-on-a-chip screens potential drug benefits or harm // National Institute of Biomedical Imaging and Bioengineering

A research team funded by the National Institutes of Health has generated a novel system for growing cardiac tissue from undifferentiated stem cells on a culture plate. This heart on a chip is a miniature physiologic system that could be used to model early heart development and screen drugs prescribed during pregnancy.

A gene called Jarid2, may play a wider role than previously thought in co-ordinating the way that stem cells change in a developing embryo to form the specialised cells that make up our bodies.

Using Low-Dose Irradiation, Researchers Can Now Edit Human Genes // Cedars-Sinai Medical Center

A simple and relevant technique, using a low level of radiation, has been shown to increase desired gene modifications in human pluripotent stem cells by an order of magnitude. This higher efficiency permits greater throughput with reduced time and cost. The method is 10 times more effective than techniques currently in use.
