Kate's Favorites

Why Gene Tests for Cancer Don't Offer More Answers // Scientific American

Despite progress, genetic profiling of tumors has a long way to go 

Superresolution intrinsic fluorescence imaging of chromatin utilizing native, unmodified nucleic acids for contrast // PNAS

OA | A Northwestern University team recently caught DNA doing something that has never been seen before: it blinked.

Research and Development

Light-driven carbon dioxide reduction to methane by nitrogenase in a photosynthetic bacterium // National Academy of Sciences

OA | The process draws on sunlight to produce methane and hydrogen inside the bacterium Rhodopseudomonas palustris, in essence reversing combustion. These engineered bacteria could guide scientists toward better carbon-neutral biofuels.

Multipotency of Adult Hippocampal NSCs In Vivo Is Restricted by Drosha/NFIB // Cell Stem Cell

Non-OA | A mechanism by which hippocampal neural stem cells regulate their own cell fate via the protein Drosha.

3K3A-activated protein C stimulates postischemic neuronal repair by human neural stem cells in mice // Nature Medicine

Non-OA | The ability of transplanted neural stem cells to ameliorate neuropathological and behavioral phenotypes after experimental stroke in mice is enhanced by co-treatment with 3K3A-APC, which acts to stimulate neuronal differentiation and functional integration within the host circuitry.

First-in-human CRISPR trial // Nature Biotechnology

Non-OA | A team of researchers from the University of Pennsylvania have received the go-ahead from a US National Institutes of Health panel for the first test that proposes using CRISPR gene editing technology in humans to treat cancer.

Peer review is a thankless task, but journals have been experimenting with accolades and cash incentives for scientists who serve as peer reviewers.
