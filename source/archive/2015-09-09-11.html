Issue #11 // Stem Cells and Regenerative Medicine Congress Special Edition

Hi everyone! I just returned from the Stem Cell Congress last week and I'm dying to share with you what I believe to be the coolest and most interesting speakers and exhibitors. Grab your afternoon beverage of choice (just a suggestion) and settle in because this is a long but must-read issue.

Cell therapy and regenerative medicine industry leaders gathered in Washington D.C. from September 2 - 3 for the 2015 annual World Stem Cells and Regenerative Medicine Congress. Designed to continue the discussion on how genomics is changing the field, as well as highlight the commercialization and manufacturing stories that have helped spur the recent successes, the meeting featured informative and inspiring keynote speeches, panel discussions, and peer-to-peer discussion groups. There was also plenty of time for sharing stories and enjoying each other's company.

For me, the highlight of the conference was the intimate size: there were only 2 rooms for talks and one for exhibitors and breaks. There were some big names at the conference, but everyone was friendly and engaging. I was lucky to speak with CEOs and researchers at the top of their field, and I learned a ton just by actively listening and soaking up their ideas at the round-table discussions. At one round-table, filled with executives and vice-presidents, everyone was discussing the difficulty of patient compliance and some tricks they learned to increase therapy compliance, one patient at a time. It was then that I realized that stem cells occupy a unique place in biotech: the stem cell industry is small, but the impact it could have is exponentially large. The Big Pharma companies are largely driven by economics, which can sometimes lead to overlooking the smaller edge-case diseases. Stem cell companies, however, are on the forefront of the precision medicine trend, largely driven by the individual nature of their science and delivery. This presents a huge opportunity for an entire class of new companies to pop up over the next few years. It truly excites me.

One example of the individual nature of stem cells comes from Kristin Comella's talk, where she described how Bioheart followed a quadriplegic patient through 8 doses of stem cell treatments. Finally, after 8 months, he could walk again. I could go on and on about the future of stem cells and the power they have, but I can't articulate this idea as well as Eric David, co-founder of Organovo. During his talk, Dr. David stressed the importance of simplifying complex organ systems to make them workable and stated the future will look decidedly different than what we can imagine. Towards the end of the conference, I managed to score a short but enlightening interview with Dr. David which I'll share with you in the coming weeks!

Overall, the conference was a great success, but I'd be remiss if I didn't mention the delicious food - the Mandarin Oriental surprised even the conference organizers with their abundant and scrumptious appetizers, entrees, snacks and desserts. The bartender at the conference happy hour was very skilled, too, making any cocktail concoction we threw at him. And trust me.... we tested him.

All in all, the event was a blast of both fun and scientific discussion, with enough networking breaks in between to collect about 42 business cards new friends. Next year the meeting is expanding and will be called the World Precision Medicine Congress. It will be held in D.C. on November 9 - 10, 2016. I highly recommend attending if you can. But on the off-chance that you can't, you can be sure that I'll be there covering the best parts once again. So stay tuned and happy sciencing!

Keynotes

Robert Hariri // Founder and CEO, Celgene and Human Longevity, Inc.

CEO of one of the largest therapeutic companies in the world, Dr. Hariri opened the conference by discussing the importance of precision medicine, and how it will improve our understanding of the factors that cause people to respond to treatments. He presented a very progressive view of the future of medicine as an integration between genetic sequencing, machine-learning algorithms (i.e. pharmacogenomics), and regenerative medicine. This new direction toward precision medicine can be summed up in a quote from Cade Hildreth: "We are not made of drugs, we are made of cells."

The opening keynote set the tone for the rest of the conference: fast-paced and jam-packed with innovative ideas and sound science. I recommend checking out his presentation slide deck to get a taste of what it was like.

Michael D. West Ph.D. // Biotime, Inc.

Dr. West's presentation was a bit different from Hariri's. While Hariri sounded like an entrepreneur-scientist giving us a glimpse of the future he's building, West taught us about the mechanics of aging with a succinct and fascinating keynote. He started with the beginning of life and the differentiation of germ cells and somatic cells, and took us up to the contemporary work on telomeres. He quoted August Weismann's work a few times. He did a fantastic job of grounding all of the contemporary aging research in an empirical and logical lineage of scientific triumph. He certainly did convince me that real anti-aging therapies could see the light of day in the next 50 years.

Top Speakers

Dr. Eric David, Co-founder and CSO // Organovo

Organovo is a 3D bioprinting company, but Dr. David began speaking to us with a series of surprising and laughable quotes from unnamed industry leaders expressing confusion, questioning necessity, and even breaching indignation towards the stem cell and regenerative medicine industry. After working out some of the issues, he jumped right into a high-level explanation of the printing process, working with primary cells to create a bioink and printing up to 1mm thick tissues with no evidence of central necrosis. The key to their expanding success is their philosophy of simplifying that complex liver organ. Dr. David asked us if we thought it possible to get 90% liver functionality with only 3 to 4 (out of 42) cell types. I deferred to him, and it turns out that you CAN. Who knew?

Dr. Devyn Smith, Head of Strategy for Pharmatherapeutics and Worldwide Research and Development // Pfizer

The conference producer told me this is the talk most people came here to see . As the only Big Pharma member in attendance, Dr. Smith broke down for us "Why invest in Regenerative Medicine in the U.S.?" The biggest considerations for any drug and treatment are manufacturing, price v.s. cost-of-goods, and payers and providers as stakeholders. Breakthrough therapies are changing healthcare and there is little room for products showing incremental benefit. It is also important, from a business perspective, to optimize the business model by patient type. Pfizer has been in cell therapy since 2009 with a few products, but Dr. Devyn is still waiting for that home-run cell based product with stellar phase II clinical trial data. If that's you, I would recommend setting up a meeting.

Dr. David J. Sharp, Professor for Department of Physiology & Biophysics and Department of Ophthalmology & Visual Sciences // Albert Einstein College of Medicine

As a professor and an entrepreneur, Dr. Sharp is all about "Harnessing the microtubule cytoskeleton to promote tissue regeneration and repair." The whole idea is to make cells move faster or change direction by harnessing the "fantastically interesting protein" fidgetin-like 2 (FL2). Knocked down using nanoparticle FL2-siRNA, cells switch to a high motility rate. To Dr. Sharp, this as a platform technology with different applications in neural regeneration of the peripheral and central nervous system as well as with cardiac tissue. Ending with cute and fantastically interesting videos of rats, Dr. Sharp showed us how FL2 promotes functional recovery 2 weeks post thoracic crush.

Dr. Kristin Comella, CSO // Bioheart

Dr. Comella's talk was about "gaining an early cash flow while developing a regenerative therapy", but the business-heavy title segued into an onslaught of medical market strategy, promising new technologies, and advice on how to deliver the maximum amount of value to your patients and customers. For example, not only is Comella the CSO of Bioheart, she also runs the the US Stem Cell Clinic and US Stem Cell Training where she helps educate practicing doctors on cutting-edge stem cell therapies.
