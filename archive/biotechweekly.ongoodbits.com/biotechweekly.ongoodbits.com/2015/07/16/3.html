Issue #3

This week in biotechnology: We have switch and mechanism discoveries abound surrounding immunity, cell size, and division. A vaccine patch with dissolving microneedles has proven useful for the flu and a promising non-invasive diabetic device has been licensed . Researchers have uncovered genes that affect blood stem cell development, and created a model of early human heart development from stem cells. Twitter came alive Monday with #RaceOnTech and Tim Flannery talks about how we consist of millions of tiny machines. Finally, I know it's not exactly biotech, but if you haven't heard, NASA got a good look at Pluto this week.

Happy Sciencing!

R&D Developments

Scientists find molecular switch that creates long-term immunity // Walter and Eliza Hall Institute of Medical Research

Melbourne researchers have identified a protein responsible for preserving the antibody-producing cells that lead to long-term immunity after infection or vaccination.

c-Myb is required for plasma cell migration to bone marrow after immunization or infection

Cell size regulation mechanism discovered // MedUni Wien

Every cell in the human body has to be a particular size in order to function correctly. The research group led by Markus Hengstschläger of the Institute for Medical Genetics at the Medical University of Vienna has now discovered a new mechanism that regulates cell size.

eIF3 controls cell size independently of S6K1-activity

New cell division mechanism discovered // Université de Montréal

Canadian and British researchers have discovered that chromosomes play an active role in animal cell division. This occurs at a precise stage, cytokinesis, where a kinetochore-based phosphatase signal and substrate are conserved. This link functions together to link anaphase chromosome movements to cortical polarization, thereby coupling chromosome segregation to cell division.

Kinetochore-localized PP1–Sds22 couples chromosome segregation to polar relaxation

Stem cells move one step closer to cure for genetic diseases // Salk Institute

Stem cells move one step closer to cure for genetic diseases. Salk scientists have created mutation-free lines of stem cells from human patients with mitochondrial diseases.

Metabolic rescue in pluripotent cells from patients with mtDNA disease

Gadgets

Could Dissolvable Microneedles Replace Injected Vaccines? // Elsevier

MicroHyala, a new microneedle patch, dissolves tiny hyaluronic acid needles with vaccine material inside once applied to the skin. Effective and easy to use without medical professionals, the hope is to use this technology in developing countries.

Clinical study and stability assessment of a novel transcutaneous influenza vaccination using a dissolving microneedle patch

Nanospheres Safely Deliver High Chemotherapy Doses in Response to Tumor Secretions // UC San Diego

Scientists have designed nanoparticles that release drugs in the presence of a class of proteins that enable cancers to metastasize. That is, they have engineered a drug delivery system so that the very enzymes that make cancers dangerous could instead guide their destruction.

Therapeutic Enzyme-Responsive Nanoparticles for Targeted Delivery and Accumulation in Tumors

Non-invasive device could end daily finger pricking for people with diabetes // University of Leeds

A new laser sensor that monitors blood glucose levels without penetrating the skin could transform the lives of millions of people living with diabetes.

The technology is licensed to Glucosense Diagnostics

Tissue Engineering

USC Stem Cell researchers poke around for blood genes // USC Stem Cell

New genes that affect blood stem cell development and maintenance have been uncovered after screening more than 100 genetic mouse strains. Different strains have different numbers of sub-populations of blood stem cells. The activation of a gene called Hopx was associated with an increased number of short-term HSCs.

The Genetic Landscape of Hematopoietic Stem Cell Frequency in Mice

Researchers create model of early human heart development from stem cells // UC Berkeley News

Berkeley researchers, in collaboration with scientists at the Gladstone Institutes, have developed a template for growing beating cardiac tissue from stem cells, creating a system that could serve as a model for early heart development and as a drug-screening tool to make pregnancies safer.

Self-organizing human cardiac microchambers mediated by geometric confinement
OPEN ACCESS

Stem Cells Might Heal Damaged Lungs // Weizmann Institute

Researchers at the Weizmann Institute simulated mouse models of lung damage and treated this with injections of new stem cells. The embryonic lung stem cells found their way through the blood to the lungs, and settled into the proper compartment. By six weeks, these cells were differentiating into normal lung tissue.

Preconditioning allows engraftment of mouse and human embryonic lung cells, enabling lung repair in mice

New Horizons Reveals Ice Mountains on Pluto // The New York Times

A day after its successful flyby, NASA’s New Horizons spacecraft sent back the first close-up photographs of Pluto, revealing a young surface dotted with ice mountains. The piano-size spacecraft traveled nine years and three billion miles to study the dwarf planet and its five moons.
