Issue #6

This week in biotechnology: We've learned how a human DNA repair enzyme works and the reasoning behind why the human heart cannot regenerate. Microbial biosensors were developed to communicate with bacteria along with nanoparticle glue for wound closure. The trick is to get that ever elusive FDA approval. Cell reprogramming using purely chemical means is leading a brighter path towards process scale-up. We also have a look at some industry trends pertaining to our lives, and some analysis on reversing the ageing process.

Happy Sciencing!

Research

Why the human heart cannot regenerate // FAU

The ability of most cardiac muscle cells to reproduce disappears in humans and all other mammals shortly after birth. FAU researchers speculate that the centrosome in cardiac muscle cells undergoes a process of disassembly which is completed shortly after birth, causing the cell to lose its ability to reproduce.

Developmental alterations in centrosome integrity contribute to the post-mitotic state of mammalian cardiomyocytes OA

Pincer attack // LMU Munich

LMU researchers have explained for the first time in detail how a human DNA repair enzyme works: First the scaffold is cracked, then defective parts are removed.

A Base-Independent Repair Mechanism for DNA Glycosylase—No Discrimination Within the Active Site OA

Understanding the molecular mechanism leading to addiction in humans // Douglas Mental Health University Institute

El Mestikawy and his colleagues found that in order to regulate our reward behaviours, a small population of neurons in our reward centre are able to respond with both “yes” and “no”.

The absence of VGLUT3 predisposes to cocaine abuse by increasing dopamine and glutamate signaling in the nucleus accumbens.

Potential new therapy approaches to reverse kidney damage identified // MD Anderson Cancer Center

Adults who are worried or terrified sometimes curl up into a fetal position. Likewise, adult cells that are injured, including genetic injury leading to cancer, initiate a process that was present during embryonic development.

Tissue Engineering

Chemical-only cell reprogramming transforms human and mouse skin cells into neurons // EurekAlert! Science News

Two independent labs have transformed skin cells into neurons using only a cocktail of chemicals. The two studies reinforce the idea that a purely chemical approach is a promising way to scale up cell reprogramming research that may avoid the technical challenges and safety concerns associated with the more popular method of using transcription factors.

Direct Conversion of Normal and Alzheimer’s Disease Human Fibroblasts into Neuronal Cells by Small Molecules OA

Small-Molecule-Driven Direct Reprogramming of Mouse Fibroblasts into Functional Neurons OA

Enthusiasm for Personalized Medicine is Premature // Columbia University Mailman School of Public Health

Increasing national focus on personalized or ‘precision’ medicine is misguided, distracting from broader investments to reduce health inequities and address the social factors that affect population health, two leading public health scholars argue in the New England Journal of Medicine.

Perspective: Public Health in the Precision-Medicine Era OA

Singapore scientists discover molecular cell cycle clock // A*STAR

Further evidence of how the differentiation of pluripotent cells is tied to and controlled by the cell cycle clock. This deeper understanding of how cells become differentiated is extremely important when considering therapeutic potentials.
